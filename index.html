Epigenetic Clocks

✓

Background — Dietary Indices
Two dietary lenses — related, but not the same
Exposure 01
HEI-2020
Healthy Eating Index
Scores alignment with the 2020–2025 Dietary Guidelines across 13 components
Higher score = better nutritional adequacy
Does not capture whether the diet is pro- or anti-inflammatory
A person can have a high HEI and a high DII simultaneously
Exposure 02
DII
Dietary Inflammatory Index
Scores the inflammatory potential of the diet based on 45 food parameters
Higher score = more pro-inflammatory; negative = anti-inflammatory
Directly tied to IL-6, CRP, and TNF-α
Does not score broad nutritional adequacy
A person can score well on one and poorly on the other —
that's the whole argument for testing them together.
Krebs-Smith et al., 2018 (HEI-2020) · Shivappa et al., 2014 (DII)
Background — Literature Review
What the literature tells us — and where it stops
Xie et al., 2023
DII vs. biological age and PhenoAge — NHANES 1999–2018. Found 0.07-year increase in biological age and 0.42-year increase in PhenoAge per DII unit. Outcome: PhenoAge, not KDM.
Xu et al., 2025
DII vs. PhenoAge directly — NHANES. DII associated with accelerated PhenoAge after full adjustment. Outcome: PhenoAge, not KDM.
Kresovich et al., 2022
Four healthy eating indices vs. four epigenetic clocks — Sister Study. Associations depended on which clock was used. Outcome: epigenetic clocks, not KDM. HEI not tested against KDM.
Chen et al., 2024
HEI, DASH, Mediterranean scores vs. DunedinPACE — WHI (n ≈ 4,500). Diet quality associated with slower pace of aging. Outcome: DunedinPACE, not KDM. HEI and DII not in the same model.
None used KDM-based biological age as the outcome. None put HEI-2020 and DII in the same model against the same outcome.
Xie et al., 2023 · Xu et al., 2025 · Kresovich et al., 2022 · Chen et al., 2024
Study Objectives
Research aims
Aim 1
Total dietary index scores
Examine whether HEI-2020 and DII total scores are each independently associated with KDM biological age deviation — and whether each contributes unique information that the other does not
Aim 2
Component decomposition
Decompose total scores into individual dietary components (~41 models) to identify which specific foods and nutrients drive the aging signal across organ systems. FDR correction at q < 0.05.
Hypothesis
Both higher diet quality and lower dietary inflammatory potential are independently associated with slower biological aging — but they capture distinct dietary dimensions, with DII more strongly predicting inflammation-mediated aging and HEI capturing broader nutritional adequacy effects.
Primary outcome: KDM biological age deviation · NHANES 2007–2012

Methods — Variables
Exposures, outcome, and covariates
Exposures
HEI-2020
Population ratio method · 2-day averaged 24-hr recall
Score 0–100; higher = better diet quality
DII
28 parameters from NHANES dietary data
Higher = more pro-inflammatory; negative = anti-inflammatory
Outcome
KDM Biological Age Deviation
Residual from survey-weighted regression of KDM biological age on chronological age
Positive → biology looks older than chronological age
Negative → biology looks younger
"Deviation" — not "acceleration." This is a point-in-time difference, not a rate of change.
Covariates
Age (continuous, years) Sex (male / female) Race / ethnicity (5 categories) Education (3 levels) Income-to-poverty ratio BMI* (continuous, kg/m²) Smoking status (never / former / current) Physical activity (federal guidelines: yes/no) Total energy intake (kcal/day)
*BMI may partially mediate the diet–aging relationship — cross-sectional limitation
BioAge package in R · Reference population: adults without diabetes, CVD, or cancer · 2007–2012 analytic sample
Methods — Statistical Analysis
Analytical approach
1
Separate models
HEI-2020 vs. BAI deviation, fully adjusted. Then DII vs. BAI deviation, fully adjusted. Establishes independent associations before entering both indices together.
2
Mutually adjusted model — the core test of Aim 1
Both indices enter the same model. Does each predict aging beyond what the other accounts for? This is the head-to-head comparison.
3
VIF check for multicollinearity
Variance inflation factors assessed when both indices are in the same model. Addresses the concern that HEI-2020 and DII may be collinear.
4
Component decomposition — Aim 2
~41 models replacing total scores with individual dietary components. Which specific foods and nutrients drive the aging signal? FDR correction at q < 0.05.

Appraisal
Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
Nationally representative sample
NHANES complex survey design with pooled 6-year weights — findings generalize to the U.S. civilian noninstitutionalized population
Novel exposure-outcome combination
First study to test HEI-2020 and DII together against a KDM-based biological age outcome — fills a specific gap in the literature
Multi-system outcome visibility
KDM keeps individual biomarker weights visible — hepatic, renal, pulmonary, and metabolic contributions remain interpretable
Analytically meaningful sensitivity design
8 vs. 9-biomarker comparison isolates the CRP-mediated inflammatory pathway — a mechanistic test, not just a robustness check
Limitations
Cross-sectional design
Point-in-time deviation, not a rate of change. Causal direction cannot be established — this is why the outcome is "deviation," not "acceleration"
Dietary recall measurement error
Two 24-hour recalls may not fully reflect long-term dietary patterns — attenuation bias toward the null is possible
BMI as covariate
BMI may partially mediate the diet–aging relationship — the cross-sectional design cannot resolve this ambiguity
Population scope
NHANES excludes institutionalized and incarcerated individuals — populations carrying elevated disease burden
