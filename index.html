Background
Aging is the shared risk factor. Chronological age is a poor predictor.
1
Chronic disease burden is rising with age
Heart disease, cancer, type 2 diabetes, and Alzheimer's share aging as their biggest underlying risk factor. Healthcare costs tied to these conditions rise as the population gets older.
2
Chronological age does not predict who develops disease
Biological aging research asks what is speeding up or slowing down physiological decline — independent of the calendar. The focus shifts from treating individual diseases to measuring the aging process itself.
3
Diet is the logical exposure
People eat every day. Dietary patterns can be changed. Evidence links nutrition to specific disease outcomes. The question is whether diet affects aging across multiple organ systems at once rather than one condition at a time.
Research question
Are diet quality and dietary inflammatory potential independently associated with biological age residuals across multiple organ systems? Do they capture distinct dietary dimensions of aging?
Cross-sectional design — association, not causation

Background — Conceptual Framework
Chronological age versus biological age
Chronological age
Your position on the time axis. Fixed. Two people born the same year occupy the same slice.
Biological age
The physiological state of that slice. Two people on the same slice can look very different biologically.
The residual
The difference between position on the time axis and the physiological state of the slice. This is what the study measures.
Klemera & Doubal, 2006 · Levine, 2013 · Miner et al., 2023

Background — Dietary Indices
Dietary exposure variables: HEI-2020 and DII
Exposure 01
HEI-2020
Healthy Eating Index
Scores alignment with the 2020–2025 Dietary Guidelines across 13 components
Higher score = better nutritional adequacy
Does not capture whether the diet is pro- or anti-inflammatory
A person can have a high HEI and a high DII simultaneously
Exposure 02
DII
Dietary Inflammatory Index
Scores the inflammatory potential of the diet based on up to 45 food parameters; 28 are available in NHANES dietary recall data
Higher score = more pro-inflammatory; negative = anti-inflammatory
Directly tied to IL-6, CRP, and TNF-α
Does not score broad nutritional adequacy
A person can score well on one index and poorly on the other. Examining both in the same model tests whether they capture redundant or distinct information about biological aging.
Krebs-Smith et al., 2018 (HEI-2020) · Shivappa et al., 2014 (DII)
Background — Literature Review
Existing literature and the methodological gap
Xie et al., 2023
DII vs. biological age and PhenoAge — NHANES 1999–2018. Found 0.07-year increase in biological age and a 0.42-year increase in phenotypic age per DII unit. Outcome: PhenoAge, not KDM.
Xu et al., 2025
DII vs. PhenoAge — NHANES. DII associated with accelerated PhenoAge after full adjustment. Outcome: PhenoAge, not KDM.
Kresovich et al., 2022
Four healthy eating indices vs. four epigenetic clocks — Sister Study. Associations depended on which clock was used. Outcome: epigenetic clocks, not KDM.
Chen et al., 2024
HEI, DASH, Mediterranean vs. DunedinPACE — WHI (n ≈ 4,500). Diet quality associated with slower aging. Outcome: DunedinPACE, not KDM. HEI and DII not in the same model.
The existing literature has not used KDM-based biological age as an outcome, and has not placed HEI-2020 and DII in the same model against the same biological age measure.
Xie et al., 2023 · Xu et al., 2025 · Kresovich et al., 2022 · Chen et al., 2024
Study Objectives
Specific Aim 1
This study tests whether HEI-2020 and DII are each independently associated with KDM biological age residuals in U.S. adults, and whether they capture distinct dietary dimensions of aging.
The reasoning
A high HEI-2020 score reflects nutritional adequacy. It does not mean biological age will be lower if the diet is also pro-inflammatory.
A low DII score reflects low inflammatory potential. It does not mean biological age will be lower if the diet is nutritionally inadequate.
The inflammaging framework supports this rationale. Chronic, low-grade inflammation driven by diet can cause organ deterioration regardless of whether dietary guidelines are met.
Measurement
Both indices calculated from two 24-hour dietary recalls on nonconsecutive days, averaged before scoring. HEI-2020 scored using the population ratio method. DII derived from 28 dietary parameters available in NHANES.
Hypothesis
Both higher diet quality and lower dietary inflammatory potential are independently associated with lower biological age residuals. These indices capture distinct dietary dimensions. DII more strongly predicts inflammation-mediated aging; HEI captures broader nutritional adequacy effects.
Primary outcome: KDM biological age residual · NHANES 2007–2012 · Shivappa et al., 2014 (DII) · Krebs-Smith et al., 2018 (HEI-2020)

Methods — Statistical Analysis
Analytical approach
1
Descriptive statistics
Weighted means and standard errors for continuous variables; weighted proportions for categorical variables. Distribution of KDM biological age residuals checked for normality; log transformation applied if needed.
2
Bivariate analysis
Differences in participant characteristics across dietary index tertiles assessed using Rao-Scott chi-square tests for categorical variables and survey-weighted F-tests for continuous variables.
3
Separate models — HEI-2020 and DII independently
Each index tested against KDM biological age residuals in a fully adjusted model. Establishes independent associations before both indices enter together.
4
Mutually adjusted model
Both indices in the same model. Does each predict biological age residuals beyond what the other accounts for? VIF assessed for multicollinearity.

Appraisal
Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
Nationally representative sample
NHANES complex survey design with pooled 6-year weights — findings generalize to the U.S. civilian noninstitutionalized population
Novel exposure-outcome combination
HEI-2020 and DII have not previously been examined together against a KDM-based biological age outcome in the same model
Multi-system outcome visibility
KDM keeps individual biomarker weights visible — hepatic, renal, pulmonary, and metabolic contributions remain interpretable
Analytically meaningful sensitivity design
8 vs. 9-biomarker comparison isolates the CRP-mediated inflammatory pathway — a mechanistic test, not just a robustness check
Limitations
Cross-sectional design
Point-in-time residual — not a rate of change. Causal direction cannot be established — this is why the outcome is "residual," not "acceleration"
Dietary recall measurement error
Two 24-hour recalls may not fully reflect long-term dietary patterns — attenuation bias toward the null is possible
BMI as covariate
BMI may partially mediate the diet–aging relationship — the cross-sectional design cannot resolve this ambiguity
Population scope
NHANES excludes institutionalized and incarcerated individuals — populations carrying elevated disease burden

Summary
Study contribution and scope
What this study does
Examines the independent and combined cross-sectional associations of HEI-2020 and DII with KDM-based biological age residuals in a nationally representative sample of U.S. adults aged 30–75, using pooled NHANES 2007–2012 data.
What it does not do
This is a cross-sectional design. It cannot establish causal direction, distinguish whether poor diet quality accelerates aging or biological age influences dietary behavior, or rule out unmeasured confounding. Findings are descriptive and associative.
Methodological gap addressed
Existing literature uses PhenoAge, DunedinPACE, or epigenetic clocks as outcomes. KDM-based biological age preserves individual biomarker contributions across six physiological systems. HEI-2020 and DII have not been examined together against this outcome in the same model.
Next steps warranted
If HEI-2020 and DII show differential associations with biological age residuals, this would provide descriptive evidence supporting the rationale for longitudinal investigation — tracking whether dietary change produces corresponding changes in KDM-based biological age over time.
NHANES 2007–2012 · Cross-sectional · Descriptive and hypothesis-generating
