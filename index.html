Background
Aging is the shared risk factor. Chronological age is a poor predictor.
1
Chronic disease burden is rising with age
The leading causes of death share one underlying risk factor: aging.
2
Chronological age does not predict who develops disease
Biological aging research asks what is speeding up or slowing down physiological decline. Chronological age does not capture this.
3
Diet is the logical exposure
Diet is modifiable, measurable, and already linked to disease outcomes. Whether it shapes aging across multiple systems simultaneously is an open question.
Research question
Are diet quality and dietary inflammation independently associated with biological age residuals? Do they capture distinct pathways?
Cross-sectional design: association, not causation

Background: Conceptual Framework
Chronological age versus biological age
Chronological age is your position on the time axis. It tells you which bar you are on.
Biological age is a physiological estimate expressed in age-equivalent years. It is what the highlighted bar looks like on the inside.
The residual
Biological age minus chronological age. A positive residual means the body looks older than the calendar age predicts. A negative residual means it looks younger. This is what this study measures.
Klemera & Doubal, 2006 · Levine, 2013 · Miner et al., 2023

Background: Literature Review
Existing literature and the methodological gap
Xie et al., 2023
DII vs. biological age + PhenoAge in NHANES 1999–2018. 0.07-yr increase per DII unit. Whether the biological age outcome is KDM-derived requires verification. DII only. No HEI.
Xu et al., 2025
DII associated with accelerated PhenoAge in NHANES after full adjustment. Outcome is PhenoAge. DII only. No HEI. No KDM.
Kresovich et al., 2022
Four eating indices vs. four epigenetic clocks in the Sister Study. Associations depended on which clock was used. No DII. No KDM.
Chen et al., 2024
HEI, DASH, Mediterranean vs. DunedinPACE in WHI (n≈4,500). Diet quality associated with slower aging. No DII. No KDM.
Gap 1
Based on my review, no study has used KDM-based biological age as an outcome with a dietary exposure.
Gap 2
I have not identified a study entering both HEI and DII in the same model against any biological aging outcome.
This study is designed to address both gaps:
KDM as the outcome, with HEI-2020 and DII
as exposures in the same model.

Study Objectives
Specific Aim 1: Total dietary index scores
Aim
Do HEI-2020 and DII each independently predict KDM biological age residuals? Does each contribute information the other does not?
Rationale
A high HEI does not guarantee low inflammatory burden. A low DII does not guarantee nutritional adequacy. Chronic inflammation can deteriorate organs regardless of whether guidelines are met.
Measurement
Two 24-hour dietary recalls on nonconsecutive days, averaged before scoring. HEI-2020: population ratio method. DII: 28 parameters from NHANES.
Hypothesis
Both indices independently predict lower biological age residuals and capture distinct dietary dimensions: DII via inflammatory pathways, HEI via broader nutritional adequacy.
Primary outcome: KDM biological age residual · NHANES 2007–2012 · Shivappa et al., 2014 (DII) · Krebs-Smith et al., 2018 (HEI-2020)

Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
Nationally representative sample
NHANES complex survey design with pooled 6-year weights; findings generalize to the U.S. civilian noninstitutionalized population
Novel exposure-outcome combination
HEI-2020 and DII have not previously been tested together against a KDM outcome
Multi-system outcome visibility
KDM keeps individual biomarker weights visible; hepatic, renal, pulmonary, and metabolic contributions remain interpretable
Analytically meaningful sensitivity design
8 vs. 9-biomarker comparison isolates the CRP-mediated inflammatory pathway; this is a mechanistic test, not a robustness check
Limitations
Cross-sectional design
Point-in-time residual, not a rate of change. Causal direction cannot be established. This is why the outcome is "residual," not "acceleration"
Dietary recall measurement error
Two 24-hour recalls may not fully reflect long-term dietary patterns; attenuation bias toward the null is possible
BMI as covariate
BMI may partially mediate the diet–aging relationship. Cannot resolve in this design.
Population scope
NHANES excludes institutionalized and incarcerated individuals; these populations carry elevated disease burden
NHANES 2007–2012 · Primary: 8-biomarker KDM · Sensitivity: 9-biomarker + CRP · 2007–2010
Summary
Study contribution and scope
What this study does
Tests whether HEI-2020 and DII are each independently associated with KDM biological age residuals in U.S. adults aged 30–75 (NHANES 2007–2012).
What it does not do
Cross-sectional design. Cannot establish causal direction or rule out unmeasured confounding. Findings are descriptive and associative.
Methodological gap addressed
The existing literature uses PhenoAge or epigenetic clocks. KDM preserves organ-system contributions. HEI-2020 and DII have not been tested together against this outcome.
Next steps warranted
If HEI-2020 and DII show differential associations with biological age residuals, this would provide descriptive evidence supporting the rationale for longitudinal investigation: tracking whether dietary change produces corresponding changes in KDM-based biological age over time.
NHANES 2007–2012 · Cross-sectional · Descriptive and hypothesis-generating
